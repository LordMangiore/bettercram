# MCAT Study Flashcards Knowledge Base

This knowledge base contains 817 MCAT study flashcards organized by category.

## Psychology/Sociology (150 cards)

### Q1: What is counterbalancing and what is an appropriate justification for using it in experimental design?
**Answer:** Counterbalancing is a method to control for any effect that the order of presenting stimuli might have on the dependent variable. Appropriate justifications include preventing participant fatigue that could cause performance decline and controlling for differential effects of exciting vs. boring tasks on subsequent responses.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q2: What is NOT an appropriate justification for counterbalancing?
**Answer:** Ensuring all participants are presented with the same variables is not appropriate. Counterbalancing is pertinent to the ORDER in which stimuli are presented, not WHAT stimuli are presented.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q3: How do positive and negative symptoms of schizophrenia differ?
**Answer:** Negative symptoms are where normal behaviors or emotions are absent. Positive symptoms are where abnormal behaviors or emotions are added, such as hallucinations and delusions.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q4: What is implicit memory and how do positive and negative priming affect it?
**Answer:** Implicit memory is nondeclarative memory involving unconscious skills like riding a bike. Negative priming results in slower response times and more errors, while positive priming results in decreased response time and decreased error rate.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q5: How does explicit memory differ from implicit memory?
**Answer:** Explicit memory is declarative memory requiring conscious recall, including episodic memory (life experiences) and semantic memory (facts not related to personal experiences). Implicit memory is nondeclarative and unconscious, like procedural skills.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q6: What is fluid intelligence and how does it differ from other cognitive abilities?
**Answer:** Fluid intelligence is the ability to think adaptively and solve problems using deductive and inductive reasoning. It involves figuring out novel situations, like navigating a new video game world, and is key to cognitive function.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q7: How do bottom-up and top-down processing differ in their approach to information processing?
**Answer:** Bottom-up processing is inductive and data-driven, using parallel processing to analyze pieces and build the whole picture. Top-down processing is deductive and conceptually driven, starting with the big picture and working down to components.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q8: What effect do neuroleptics have on schizophrenia symptoms?
**Answer:** Neuroleptics help treat the positive symptoms of schizophrenia. However, by dulling positive symptoms, they may exacerbate negative symptoms.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q9: What is the difference between implicit and explicit memory in terms of processing speed and accuracy?
**Answer:** Implicit (nondeclarative) memory results in slower speed and more errors compared to explicit memory. Implicit memory operates unconsciously, while explicit memory involves conscious recall of information.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q10: What are the three stages of General Adaptation Syndrome and what happens in each?
**Answer:** Alarm stage activates the sympathetic nervous system. Resistance stage involves the body maintaining increased hormones to cope with stress. Exhaustion stage occurs when the body can no longer maintain increased sympathetic activity, leading to increased illness susceptibility or even death.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q11: According to the activation-synthesis model, why do dreams feel both bizarre and familiar?
**Answer:** Dreams result from widespread, random activation of neural circuitry that mimics sensory information, memories, and unmet needs. The cortex attempts to stitch this random information together, creating dreams that are bizarre yet familiar.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q12: What is the difference between dependent and independent stressors?
**Answer:** Dependent stressors result from an individual's own behavior (like excessive reassurance seeking leading to isolation). Independent stressors are outside the individual's control (like natural disasters, family deaths, or car accidents).
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q13: What type of visual information does parallel processing handle, and does it require conscious effort?
**Answer:** Parallel processing handles visual information like color, form, motion, depth, as well as space, time, and frequency of events. It does not require conscious effort and automatically processes routine, familiar information across multiple channels.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q14: What happens when a split-brain patient sees the word 'ball' in the right visual field and 'room' in the left visual field?
**Answer:** The patient would only verbally respond 'ball'. The right visual field connects to the left hemisphere where language areas are located, allowing verbalization. The left visual field connects to the right hemisphere, but without the corpus callosum, this information cannot reach the language centers in the left hemisphere.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q15: How does hemi-spatial neglect differ from split-brain syndrome?
**Answer:** Hemi-spatial neglect results from damage to the right parietal lobe and causes an attentional/awareness deficit where patients ignore stimuli on the left side. Split-brain syndrome results from severing the corpus callosum and prevents communication between hemispheres while maintaining normal processing within each hemisphere.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q16: What are the age ranges for Piaget's four stages of cognitive development?
**Answer:** Sensorimotor stage: 0-2 years; Preoperational stage: 2-7 years; Concrete operational stage: 7-11 years; Formal operational stage: 12+ years. Each stage represents increasingly sophisticated cognitive abilities and ways of understanding the world.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q17: What are the three key assumptions of symbolic interactionist perspective?
**Answer:** 1) Humans act toward symbols based on the meanings those symbols carry; 2) The meanings of symbols come from social interaction; 3) Humans interpret the meaning of symbols and this interpretation influences their actions. This perspective emphasizes interpretation and response in human behavior.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q18: How does exchange theory (rational choice theory) explain decision-making in relationships?
**Answer:** Exchange theory suggests people weigh costs and benefits to maximize their benefits when making decisions. In relationships, people consider what benefits they receive, seek balance and reciprocity, and rank their options to choose the most beneficial outcome. This is a micro- and meso-level theory.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q19: How do conflict theory and structural functionalism differ in their view of society?
**Answer:** Conflict theory (macro-level) sees inevitable conflicts between groups with unequal power, like wealthy capitalists versus working class. Structural functionalism is the inverse - it views groups as having complementary roles that work together to maintain social equilibrium and stability.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q20: What is the difference between adaptive and maladaptive coping strategies?
**Answer:** Adaptive coping involves healthy strategies like support-seeking and reaching out to loved ones or friends. Maladaptive coping involves unhealthy strategies like using drugs or alcohol to deal with stress.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q21: How do Bipolar I and Bipolar II disorders differ in their diagnostic criteria?
**Answer:** Bipolar I requires at least one manic episode (may or may not include depressive episodes). Bipolar II requires at least one hypomanic episode AND at least one major depressive episode, but NEVER a manic episode.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q22: What psychological concept describes the belief in one's ability to perform behaviors that lead to expected outcomes?
**Answer:** Self-efficacy. This refers to an individual's confidence in their capability to execute specific behaviors necessary to produce particular performance attainments. It influences motivation, effort, and persistence in pursuing goals.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q23: What are heuristics and what is a key clue phrase that indicates their use?
**Answer:** Heuristics are cognitive rules of thumb that offer shortcuts to reasoning and problem solving. The key clue phrase is 'making quick judgements,' which indicates the use of these mental shortcuts rather than systematic analysis.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q24: How does attachment theory explain the development of self-esteem?
**Answer:** According to attachment theory, self-esteem reflects an individual's history of rejection and acceptance in family and friend relationships. Early attachments with primary caregivers significantly influence later behavior and self-perception throughout life.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q25: What is the halo effect and how does it influence social judgments?
**Answer:** The halo effect occurs when judgments about a specific aspect of a person are influenced by one's overall impression of that person. For example, attractive individuals are more likely to be perceived as trustworthy and friendly, demonstrating how one positive trait influences perception of other traits.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q26: How does social facilitation relate to the Yerkes-Dodson law?
**Answer:** Social facilitation occurs when audience presence improves performance, but this effect depends on task complexity. People perform better on simple, well-known tasks when being watched, but worse on complex, unfamiliar tasks, illustrating the Yerkes-Dodson relationship between arousal and performance.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q27: What is scaffolding in Vygotsky's theory of cognitive development?
**Answer:** Scaffolding refers to the support provided by a more experienced individual to help someone less experienced learn new skills or concepts. The support is gradually removed as the learner becomes more competent, allowing for independent mastery.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q28: What is egocentrism according to Piaget's theory?
**Answer:** Egocentrism is when a child views the world solely from their own perspective and cannot imagine what someone else may think or feel. It represents the inability to take another person's point of view or understand that others have different perspectives.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q29: What is the fundamental attribution error?
**Answer:** The fundamental attribution error is the tendency to attribute another person's behavior to their internal traits or personality rather than considering situational factors. This bias leads to overestimating the role of personality and underestimating environmental influences on behavior.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q30: How do surveys, content analysis, and qualitative interviews differ in research methodology?
**Answer:** Surveys use close-ended questions that are quantified for statistical analysis. Content analysis examines visual or literary artifacts to make inferences about social phenomena. Qualitative in-depth interviews use open-ended questions to gather detailed, subjective information.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q31: What is the difference between absolute threshold and difference threshold (just noticeable difference)?
**Answer:** Absolute threshold is the minimum stimulus intensity that an individual can detect 50% of the time. Difference threshold (just noticeable difference) is the smallest detectable difference between two stimuli that a person can perceive 50% of the time.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q32: How does the Ames room create an optical illusion that overrides size constancy?
**Answer:** The Ames room is trapezoidal but appears rectangular when viewed monocularly through a peep hole. Without binocular depth cues, the viewer cannot accurately judge distance, so two people at different distances appear to be the same distance away but dramatically different sizes, overriding the brain's size constancy mechanism.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q33: What is stereopsis and what are the two main binocular depth cues that contribute to it?
**Answer:** Stereopsis is depth perception arising from integrated information from both eyes. The two main binocular depth cues are retinal disparity (different images on each retina due to eye separation, with closer objects showing greater disparity) and convergence (angular positioning of eyes when focusing on objects).
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q34: What is convergence in vision and how does it help determine distance?
**Answer:** Convergence is the inward turning of both eyes to focus on nearby objects. The closer an object is to the observer, the more the eyes converge. The brain interprets the angle of convergence as an indication of distance from the observer.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q35: Define sensory adaptation and explain its mechanism.
**Answer:** Sensory adaptation is a diminished response by the nervous system when presented with an unchanging stimulus over time. It occurs due to decreased nervous system responsiveness, which results in diminished perception of that constant stimulus.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q36: According to Weber's law, what relationship exists between background intensity and the threshold needed to perceive a change?
**Answer:** Weber's law states that the change in intensity divided by the background intensity equals a constant ratio. This means that higher background intensities require proportionally larger changes to be perceived (linear relationship).
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q37: What is motion parallax and how does it serve as a monocular depth cue?
**Answer:** Motion parallax is the apparent difference in speed of moving objects at different distances. Objects closer to the observer appear to move faster, while objects farther away appear to move slower, helping us perceive depth and distance.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q38: What distinguishes personality disorders from other mental health conditions like mood or anxiety disorders?
**Answer:** Personality disorders involve inflexible, enduring patterns of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that are stable across time and situations, causing functional impairment. Unlike mood or anxiety disorders, they represent persistent maladaptive personality traits rather than episodic symptoms.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q39: According to Gestalt principles, what does the Law of Closure describe about human perception?
**Answer:** The Law of Closure states that objects grouped together are perceived as a whole, and the brain fills in missing parts of shapes or patterns. This allows us to perceive complete forms even when parts are missing or obscured.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q40: What is the difference between absolute threshold and just noticeable difference (JND)?
**Answer:** Absolute threshold is the minimum stimulus intensity needed to detect a stimulus 50% of the time. JND is the minimum difference between two stimuli needed to notice they are different. Absolute threshold is about detection, while JND is about discrimination.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q41: What is taste aversion and why should physicians advise patients to avoid favorite foods on chemotherapy days?
**Answer:** Taste aversion is a form of classical conditioning where foods become associated with illness (like nausea/vomiting from chemotherapy) and are avoided long-term, even if the food didn't cause the illness. Physicians should advise avoiding favorite foods on chemotherapy days to prevent patients from developing permanent aversions to foods they previously enjoyed.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q42: What is the difference between reliability and validity in research?
**Answer:** Reliability refers to consistency - reliable measures produce similar results every time and have high replicability. Validity refers to accuracy - whether results actually reflect reality. Reliability is necessary but not sufficient for validity; consistent results don't guarantee accurate results.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q43: What is external validity and why might highly controlled experiments lack it?
**Answer:** External validity (generalizability) is the extent to which study results can be applied outside the laboratory to real-life situations. Highly controlled experiments may have high reliability and internal validity but low external validity because tight control of variables may not reflect real-world conditions.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q44: What is extinction in classical conditioning?
**Answer:** Extinction occurs when a conditioned response gradually stops occurring in the absence of the unconditioned stimulus. The learned association weakens over time when the conditioned stimulus is repeatedly presented without the unconditioned stimulus.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q45: What are feature detectors and what evidence supports their existence?
**Answer:** Feature detectors are specific neurons that preferentially fire in response to very specific stimuli. The strongest evidence comes from experiments showing certain visual cortex neurons fire preferentially to specific stimuli like bars of light oriented at particular angles.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q46: What is confirmation bias and how does it affect information processing?
**Answer:** Confirmation bias is a cognitive bias where individuals favor evidence supporting their beliefs while dismissing contradictory evidence. They also interpret ambiguous information as confirmation of their existing beliefs, leading to biased reasoning.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q47: According to the learning theory of language development, how do humans acquire language?
**Answer:** The learning theory proposes that humans are born as 'blank slates' and language is a learned behavior. Language develops through environmental exposure, operant conditioning, imitation, and repeated language production.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q48: How do universalism and the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis differ regarding the relationship between language and cognition?
**Answer:** Universalism believes cognition controls language and is required for language development. The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis suggests the opposite - that language either controls cognition or merely influences it.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q49: What does structural functionalism propose about how society operates?
**Answer:** Structural functionalism is a macro-level sociological perspective suggesting that all aspects of society work together to maintain dynamic equilibrium. Each part of society serves a function in maintaining overall stability.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q50: According to the elaboration likelihood model, what factors are most persuasive when someone is uninterested in a message?
**Answer:** When an individual is uninterested in a message, superficial factors (such as attractiveness of the speaker, humor, or other peripheral cues) are most persuasive rather than the actual content or logical arguments.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q51: What brain functions are primarily associated with the right hemisphere?
**Answer:** The right hemisphere is crucial for processing visuospatial patterns, artistic and musical abilities, visualization, and emotion. Deficits in visuospatial processing often indicate right hemisphere damage.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q52: What are mirror neurons and what role do they play in learning?
**Answer:** Mirror neurons are specialized neurons that fire both while observing and while performing a behavior. They are found in various brain regions and play a central role in observational learning.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q53: What defines a meritocracy and what factors are excluded from this system?
**Answer:** A meritocracy is a system where individuals progress and earn rewards based solely on merit such as talent, ability, and effort. Factors like prestige, social connections, or other non-merit based advantages do not count in determining advancement.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q54: According to social cognitive theory, under what circumstances would people be LEAST susceptible to self-positivity bias?
**Answer:** People would be least susceptible to self-positivity bias if they have friends with the disease. This occurs because vicarious learning through observing others' experiences makes negative outcomes seem more realistic and probable.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q55: What is the key difference between central and peripheral routes in the elaboration likelihood model of persuasion?
**Answer:** The central route engages receivers through deep, meaningful processing using well-reasoned arguments and logical content. The peripheral route engages receivers superficially through methods that require little thought, and is more effective when people have low motivation or ability to process the message.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q56: According to cognitive dissonance theory, what motivates people to change their thoughts or behaviors?
**Answer:** Cognitive dissonance creates discomfort when beliefs, attitudes, or behaviors are contradictory or incompatible. This uncomfortable state motivates people to reduce the conflict by aligning their thoughts and behaviors to achieve cognitive consistency.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q57: How do self-positivity bias and optimism bias differ from actor-observer bias?
**Answer:** Self-positivity bias and optimism bias both involve underestimating personal vulnerability to negative outcomes. Actor-observer bias involves attribution differences - attributing others' behavior to internal causes while attributing one's own behavior to external factors.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q58: What distinguishes confirmation bias from overconfidence bias?
**Answer:** Confirmation bias involves seeking information that supports existing conclusions while ignoring contradictory evidence. Overconfidence bias occurs when people's subjective certainty in their beliefs exceeds the objective accuracy of those beliefs.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q59: What does the sick role theory explain about how society handles illness?
**Answer:** Sick role theory is a functionalist approach that explains how illness disruption to normal social activity is minimized by legitimizing illness as socially acceptable deviance. It describes how the sick role allows people to temporarily deviate from normal social expectations while maintaining social stability.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q60: How does the illness experience approach differ from sick role theory in understanding illness?
**Answer:** The illness experience is a symbolic interactionist approach that focuses on how individuals personally incorporate and make sense of illness as part of their self-identity and daily routines. Unlike sick role theory's focus on social function, this approach emphasizes personal meaning-making and identity formation.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q61: What is elaboration as an encoding strategy and why is it effective?
**Answer:** Elaboration is an encoding strategy where new information is meaningfully associated with previously known information. It enhances memory by creating more connections to the new material, which improves learning and makes information easier to retrieve later.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q62: What factors does the elaboration likelihood model consider when determining a message's persuasiveness?
**Answer:** The elaboration likelihood model determines persuasiveness based on how deeply or superficially a message is processed and the characteristics of the audience. Deep processing leads to more stable attitude change, while superficial processing relies on peripheral cues.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q63: How does the availability heuristic influence our judgments about probability?
**Answer:** The availability heuristic causes people to judge how common or likely something is based on how easily examples come to mind. Events that are more memorable or recent seem more probable than they actually are, leading to biased probability estimates.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q64: What is the difference between escape learning and avoidance learning in operant conditioning?
**Answer:** Escape learning occurs when an organism learns to terminate an ongoing unpleasant stimulus, while avoidance learning occurs when an organism prevents contact with an unpleasant stimulus before it begins. Both result from negative reinforcement but differ in timing relative to the aversive stimulus.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q65: What are the three key components of expectancy theory of motivation?
**Answer:** Expectancy theory includes expectancy (belief that one can achieve the desired outcome), instrumentality (belief that one has control over the outcome), and valence (the value placed on the desired outcome). All three components must be present for motivation to occur.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q66: According to arousal theory, how do people respond when they are underaroused versus overaroused?
**Answer:** When underaroused, people seek activities to increase stimulation to reach their optimum arousal level. When overaroused, people seek to decrease stimulation to return to their optimal level. This theory suggests motivation stems from maintaining balanced arousal.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q67: What is the key difference between internal and external locus of control?
**Answer:** Internal locus of control means believing your actions influence outcomes, while external locus of control means believing outcomes are due to luck or fate beyond your control. This affects how individuals approach challenges and their sense of personal agency.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q68: According to drive reduction theory, what sequence of events leads to motivated behavior?
**Answer:** Homeostasis is disrupted → biological need is generated → drive to fulfill that need develops → action is prompted. This theory suggests motivation stems from the body's attempt to restore balance and meet basic biological requirements.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q69: How are learned helplessness, self-efficacy, and locus of control related?
**Answer:** Learned helplessness is associated with low self-efficacy and external locus of control. When people repeatedly fail and feel powerless, they develop beliefs that they cannot influence outcomes (external locus) and lack confidence in their abilities (low self-efficacy).
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q70: What happens when the corpus callosum is severed, and how does it affect visual processing?
**Answer:** Severing the corpus callosum disconnects the brain hemispheres, commonly done to treat severe epilepsy. Information in the left visual field is processed by the right hemisphere, so patients cannot verbally describe what they see but can draw it since drawing doesn't require language.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q71: What characterizes a newly industrializing society in terms of demographic transition?
**Answer:** A newly industrializing society is characterized by decreasing death rates and increasing access to food, medicine, and improved sanitation. This represents a stage in demographic transition where mortality declines due to improved living conditions and healthcare infrastructure.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q72: What is social loafing and what is its primary consequence?
**Answer:** Social loafing occurs when an individual exerts less effort while working in a group versus working alone. This phenomenon often translates into a loss of group productivity as members rely on others to carry the workload.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q73: How do subcultures differ from countercultures in their relationship to mainstream society?
**Answer:** Subcultures have values and norms that differ somewhat from mainstream society but are not opposed to it. Unlike countercultures, subcultures are distinctive but maintain compatibility with dominant cultural values.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q74: What defines a utilitarian organization and how does it motivate participation?
**Answer:** Utilitarian organizations compensate members for their involvement through tangible rewards such as money, certificates, or diplomas. These organizations rely on material incentives rather than coercive power or normative commitment to maintain membership.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q75: What is an aggregate and how does it differ from a social group?
**Answer:** An aggregate is a collection of individuals who share a common location but do not identify as a group, such as all people at a store at the same time. Unlike social groups, aggregates lack shared identity, interaction patterns, or common purpose.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q76: What is secularization and how does it affect religious institutions?
**Answer:** Secularization is the reduced power of religion as religious involvement declines in society. As this process occurs, religious institutions lose social and political influence, diminishing their role in public life and decision-making.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q77: How does religiosity differ from general religious affiliation?
**Answer:** Religiosity refers to the extent to which a religious doctrine is internalized and incorporated into an individual's life. It measures the depth of religious commitment and practice, not just nominal membership or belief.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q78: What is medicalization and what are its consequences for medical professionals' authority?
**Answer:** Medicalization is the process of defining human behaviors or characteristics as medical conditions, often due to shifting attitudes or new scientific evidence. As medicalization increases, medical professionals gain more power and authority as society increasingly relies on them as legitimate sources of guidance on lifestyle behaviors.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q79: What is teacher expectancy and what mechanism drives this phenomenon?
**Answer:** Teacher expectancy occurs when a teacher's beliefs about a student result in the student meeting those expectations. This is thought to occur as a result of the teacher's differential behavior toward the student based on their preconceived notions.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q80: What is the hidden curriculum and how does it reinforce social values?
**Answer:** The hidden curriculum includes implied, informal mechanisms by which certain values and behaviors are promoted in educational settings. For example, a teacher calling mainly on male students in math class reinforces gender stereotypes about mathematical ability.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q81: What is the representativeness heuristic and how can it affect medical decision-making?
**Answer:** The representativeness heuristic is the tendency to compare things to mental prototypes when making judgments. In medicine, this can lead to delayed treatment when patients' symptoms don't match 'prototypical' presentations seen in media or training.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q82: What is the DIFFERENCE between appropriate and inappropriate justifications for counterbalancing in experimental design?
**Answer:** APPROPRIATE: Control for order effects like fatigue, practice effects, or carry-over effects between conditions (e.g., participants becoming fatigued or one task affecting performance on subsequent tasks). INAPPROPRIATE: Ensuring all participants receive the same stimuli - this confuses counterbalancing (which controls ORDER of presentation) with standardization (which controls WHAT is presented).
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q83: A patient with schizophrenia is prescribed neuroleptics and shows improvement in hallucinations and delusions, but family reports increased social withdrawal and flat affect. What explains this treatment paradox?
**Answer:** Neuroleptics effectively treat POSITIVE symptoms (hallucinations, delusions) but may exacerbate NEGATIVE symptoms (absent normal behaviors/emotions like social withdrawal, flat affect). The medication's mechanism that reduces abnormal additions may also suppress normal emotional and behavioral responses.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q84: What is the DIFFERENCE between negative and positive priming effects in implicit memory, and how do they manifest behaviorally?
**Answer:** NEGATIVE PRIMING: Results in SLOWER response times and MORE errors when processing previously ignored stimuli. POSITIVE PRIMING: Results in FASTER response times and FEWER errors when processing previously encountered stimuli. Both occur unconsciously as part of implicit/nondeclarative memory systems.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q85: If a researcher wants to test whether bottom-up or top-down processing is being used, which experimental manipulation would be most appropriate?
**Answer:** Present ambiguous stimuli with varying amounts of contextual information. Bottom-up processing (inductive, data-driven, parallel processing) would rely more on analyzing individual components regardless of context. Top-down processing (deductive, concept-driven) would be heavily influenced by contextual clues and prior expectations.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q86: A patient shows impaired verbal fluency and increased errors on priming tasks. These symptoms are MOST consistent with dysfunction in which memory system and are characteristic of which disorders?
**Answer:** IMPLICIT (nondeclarative) memory dysfunction, specifically negative priming effects. This pattern is characteristic of both Alzheimer's disease and schizophrenia, which share cognitive dysfunction including impaired verbal fluency (ability to retrieve information within restricted parameters) and negative priming deficits.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q87: During the exhaustion stage of General Adaptation Syndrome, which of the following would NOT be expected?
**Answer:** Continued high sympathetic nervous system activation. During exhaustion, the body can NO LONGER maintain the increased sympathetic activity from the resistance stage, leading to decreased immune function, increased illness susceptibility, and potential cardiovascular problems or death. The sympathetic system becomes depleted, not hyperactive.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q88: How does the activation-synthesis model explain the paradox of dreams being both bizarre yet familiar?
**Answer:** Random neural activation creates bizarre combinations, but the cortex tries to synthesize this activity using familiar stored memories, desires, and sensory information. This creates dreams that feel familiar (due to real memory components) yet bizarre (due to random neural firing patterns that don't match normal sensory input).
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q89: What is the DIFFERENCE between fluid intelligence and the type of memory formation involved in long-term potentiation?
**Answer:** Fluid intelligence is the ability to think adaptively and solve novel problems using reasoning (like navigating new environments). Long-term potentiation is the neurobiological mechanism of memory formation through strengthening neural connections via repeated use - it's the physical basis for learning, not the cognitive ability itself.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q90: If an MCAT question states that a memory task 'MIGHT be' related to explicit memory, why would this answer choice be incorrect?
**Answer:** MCAT answers must be definitively true based on given information, not just possible. Explicit memory is DECLARATIVE memory requiring conscious recall (episodic life experiences, semantic facts). If something only 'might be' explicit memory, it doesn't meet the certainty threshold required for MCAT answers - the relationship must be clearly established.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q91: A patient with a severed corpus callosum is shown the word 'APPLE' in their left visual field and 'ORANGE' in their right visual field. What would you expect them to verbally report?
**Answer:** They would only say 'ORANGE'. The left visual field crosses to the right hemisphere, but language areas are typically in the left hemisphere. Since the corpus callosum is severed, the right hemisphere cannot communicate the 'APPLE' information to the left hemisphere's language centers. Only 'ORANGE' (right visual field → left hemisphere) can be verbalized.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q92: What is the KEY difference between hemi-spatial neglect and split-brain syndrome?
**Answer:** Hemi-spatial neglect is an attentional/awareness deficit caused by right parietal lobe damage, resulting in left-side neglect. Split-brain is caused by severed corpus callosum, preventing interhemispheric communication but leaving each hemisphere's processing intact. Neglect = can't attend to one side; Split-brain = hemispheres can't communicate.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q93: A researcher studies how wealthy corporations exploit workers through wage suppression. Which sociological theory framework are they MOST likely using, and at what level?
**Answer:** Conflict theory at the macro level. This theory focuses on inevitable conflicts between groups with unequal power (wealthy class vs. proletariat), particularly in capitalist systems where the wealthy control means of production. This is macro-level because it examines large-scale societal structures.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q94: What is the CRITICAL diagnostic difference between Bipolar I and Bipolar II disorders regarding manic episodes?
**Answer:** Bipolar I: Must have at least ONE full manic episode (may or may not have depressive episodes). Bipolar II: Must have hypomanic episodes AND major depressive episodes, but NEVER a full manic episode. The presence of even one manic episode automatically makes it Bipolar I.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q95: If a patient constantly seeks reassurance from others about their health despite normal test results, what type of stressor are they likely creating, and how does this differ from losing a job due to company downsizing?
**Answer:** The reassurance-seeking creates DEPENDENT stressors (negative life events influenced by their behavior, like social isolation). Job loss due to downsizing is an INDEPENDENT stressor (external events like natural disasters, not influenced by personal behavior). Dependent = self-influenced; Independent = external/uncontrollable.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q96: A 9-year-old child can understand that 5+3=8 but cannot solve hypothetical problems like 'If all birds were blue, and this animal is a bird, what color would it be?' Which Piaget stages explain this limitation?
**Answer:** The child is in the concrete operational stage (ages 7-11), where they can perform logical operations on concrete, tangible objects but cannot handle abstract, hypothetical reasoning. The hypothetical problem requires formal operational thinking (12+), which involves abstract reasoning and 'what-if' scenarios.
**Difficulty:** Advanced

### Q97: According to exchange theory, why might someone stay in a relationship where they give more than they receive, and what level of analysis does this theory operate at?

### Q107: What is the DIFFERENCE between retinal disparity and convergence as binocular depth cues?

### Q109: What is the DIFFERENCE between sensory adaptation and the concept of Just Noticeable Difference (JND)?

Adaptation affects baseline sensitivity; JND measures discrimination ability.

### Q1: What is long-term potentiation and how does it relate to memory?
**Answer:** Long-term potentiation is the strengthening of neural connections through repeated use. It functions like long-term memory, creating lasting changes in the brain that allow information to stick with us for extended periods.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q2: What distinguishes the lytic cycle from the lysogenic cycle in bacteriophages?
**Answer:** The lytic cycle involves immediate infection where the bacteriophage fills the cell with progeny until it bursts (lyses). The lysogenic cycle involves integration of viral genome into bacterial DNA where it replicates along with the host cell.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q3: What are prions and what diseases do they cause?
**Answer:** Prions are infectious proteins that trigger misfolding of normal proteins from α-helices to β-pleated sheets, interfering with cell function. They cause mad cow disease, familial fatal insomnia, and Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q4: What does the neutral theory of molecular evolution propose about genetic mutations?
**Answer:** The neutral theory proposes that most genetic mutations are neutral and don't affect reproductive success. These neutral mutations accumulate at a constant rate over time through genetic drift, forming the basis for molecular clocks used to estimate evolutionary relationships between species.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q5: What is incomplete dominance and how does it differ from complete dominance?
**Answer:** Incomplete dominance occurs when a heterozygous offspring displays an intermediate phenotype that blends both parental traits. For example, a red flower crossed with a white flower produces pink offspring. This differs from complete dominance where one allele masks the expression of another.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q6: How can a eukaryotic organism reduce chromosome number without losing genetic information?
**Answer:** Two mechanisms can achieve this: (1) end-to-end fusion of two chromosomes with inactivation of one centromere, or (2) chromosomal breakage at the centromere followed by fusion of each portion to other chromosome ends. Both transfer intact genetic material to different chromosomes without information loss.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q7: What are the main noncoding regions of eukaryotic chromosomes and their functions?
**Answer:** The centromere facilitates spindle fiber attachment during cell division, while telomeres protect chromosomal ends from degradation. These regions don't contain protein-coding genes but are essential for chromosome stability and proper segregation.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q8: How does calcium regulation by the sarcoplasmic reticulum control muscle contraction?
**Answer:** The sarcoplasmic reticulum sequesters calcium during relaxation and releases it into the cytosol during contraction in response to action potentials. Released calcium binds to troponin, allowing actin and myosin filaments to slide across each other and generate muscle contraction.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q9: Describe the sequence of events at the neuromuscular junction from action potential to muscle contraction.
**Answer:** An action potential at the motor neuron terminal triggers ACh release via exocytosis. ACh diffuses across the synaptic cleft and binds to nicotinic receptors on the muscle fiber, leading to muscle contraction. ACh is then degraded by acetylcholinesterase or diffuses away to terminate contraction.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q10: What are the developmental origins of the forebrain, midbrain, and hindbrain?
**Answer:** The forebrain develops from the prosencephalon and becomes the cerebrum. The midbrain develops from the mesencephalon and remains the midbrain. The hindbrain develops from the rhombencephalon and becomes the cerebellum.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q11: What is the difference between afferent and efferent neurons in terms of direction of signal transmission?
**Answer:** Afferent neurons carry signals toward the central nervous system (ascending/sensory). Efferent neurons carry signals away from the CNS toward the periphery (descending/motor). Remember: Afferent = Approaching, Efferent = Exiting.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q12: How does motor unit size relate to precision of movement, and what causes motor unit abnormalities?
**Answer:** Small motor units (few muscle fibers per neuron) provide precise control in areas like fingers and eyes, while large motor units control gross movements in legs and abdomen. Motor unit abnormalities can cause muscle weakness due to disrupted neuromuscular control.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q13: What are the four main signs of lower motor neuron dysfunction?
**Answer:** Atrophy (decreased muscle bulk), fasciculations (involuntary muscle twitches), hypotonia (decreased muscle tone), and hyporeflexia (decreased muscle stretch reflexes). These result from damage to neurons with cell bodies in the brainstem or spinal cord.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q14: Describe the pathway of a muscle stretch reflex and why it doesn't require conscious control.
**Answer:** When a muscle is stretched rapidly, afferent somatosensory neurons detect this and send signals to the spinal cord, where they synapse directly on efferent lower motor neurons that cause the muscle to contract protectively. This reflex arc stays in the lower CNS (spinal cord) and bypasses the cerebral cortex, making it involuntary.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q15: What is the corticospinal tract and why does the right brain control the left body?
**Answer:** The corticospinal tract consists of upper motor neuron axons that originate from cell bodies in the cerebral cortex, descend through the brainstem (midbrain, pons, medulla), and mostly cross over to the opposite side at the bottom of the medulla before synapsing on lower motor neurons in the spinal cord. This crossover explains contralateral control.

### Q16: How do upper motor neuron signs differ from lower motor neuron signs?
**Answer:** Upper motor neuron signs include hyperreflexia (increased reflexes), clonus (rhythmic muscle contractions), hypertonia (increased muscle tone/spasticity), and Babinski sign (toes extend upward when foot sole is stimulated). These contrast with lower motor neuron signs which show decreased reflexes, tone, and muscle bulk.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q17: What distinguishes the corticobulbar tract from the corticospinal tract?
**Answer:** The corticobulbar tract controls lower motor neurons in the brainstem that innervate head and neck muscles, while the corticospinal tract controls lower motor neurons in the spinal cord for body and limb muscles. Corticobulbar upper motor neurons may control lower motor neurons on the same or opposite side of the brainstem.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q18: What are the basic structural differences between sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems?
**Answer:** Sympathetic: originates in the middle spinal cord with short preganglionic axons synapsing on neurons with long postganglionic axons. Both control smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands.

**Difficulty:** medium

### Q28: What is the key structural and functional difference between oligodendrocytes and Schwann cells?
**Answer:** Oligodendrocytes are in the CNS and have multiple arms that can myelinate multiple neurons (one segment per axon). Schwann cells are in the PNS and only create myelin for one segment on one axon.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q29: What are ependymal cells and what is their primary function?
**Answer:** Ependymal cells form a simple cuboidal epithelium that lines structures containing CSF. They have microvilli and cilia to help move CSF around, secrete CSF, and form a leaky barrier compared to the blood-brain barrier.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q30: What is the typical resting membrane potential of a neuron and what maintains it?
**Answer:** The typical resting potential is -60 mV. It's maintained by the sodium-potassium pump, which pumps K+ into the cell and Na+ out. The membrane is very permeable to K+ and partially permeable to Na+, with more Na+ outside and more K+ inside.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q31: Describe the sequence of events during neuronal depolarization and repolarization.
**Answer:** Depolarization: Na+ channels open, Na+ flows in making the cell positive. Repolarization: Na+ inactivation gates close, K+ channels open, K+ exits making the cell negative again. This is followed by hyperpolarization as K+ channels stay open longer.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q32: What is the distribution of major ions inside vs outside neurons at rest?
**Answer:** Inside neurons: more organic anions (OA-) and K+. Outside neurons: more Na+, Cl-, and Ca2+. This distribution creates the negative resting membrane potential of approximately -60 mV.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q33: Why are organic anions (OA-) unable to cross the neuron membrane despite having a concentration gradient?
**Answer:** Organic anions are impermeable to the neuron membrane. Even though they have a concentration gradient that would drive their movement, the membrane's impermeability prevents them from crossing, maintaining the negative charge inside the cell.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q34: What is saltatory conduction and why does it occur?
**Answer:** Saltatory conduction is the rapid transmission of action potentials in myelinated axons where the signal appears to 'jump' from node to node of Ranvier. This occurs because myelination insulates the axon, forcing the action potential to propagate only at the unmyelinated nodes, resulting in faster conduction than in unmyelinated axons.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q35: What role does complexin play in neurotransmitter release, and how is it regulated?
**Answer:** Complexin acts as a 'brake' that prevents vesicles from fusing with the axon terminal membrane and releasing neurotransmitters. The vesicle protein synaptotagmin can bind and release complexin in the presence of calcium ions, allowing vesicle fusion and neurotransmitter release to occur.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q36: Describe the sequence of events that leads to neurotransmitter release at the axon terminal.
**Answer:** Action potential reaches axon terminal → positive ions flood the cell → voltage-gated calcium channels open → calcium enters the cell → calcium activates synaptotagmin → synaptotagmin releases complexin 'brake' → vesicles fuse with membrane → neurotransmitters are released into synaptic cleft.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q37: What are the major excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmitters, and how do they affect ion channels?
**Answer:** Glutamate is the major excitatory neurotransmitter and opens channels that allow positive ions (like Na+) to enter, causing depolarization. GABA is the major inhibitory neurotransmitter and opens Cl- channels, causing hyperpolarization and inhibition of the postsynaptic cell.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q38: Compare chemical synapses and electrical synapses in terms of structure.
**Answer:** Chemical synapses have a synaptic cleft (gap) between the presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons, requiring neurotransmitters to cross the gap. Electrical synapses have no gap and connect directly through gap junctions, allowing direct electrical current flow between cells.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q39: Classify the three main types of neurotransmitters and give examples of each.
**Answer:** Amino acid neurotransmitters (glutamate, GABA, glycine) have amino and carboxylic acid groups. Peptide neurotransmitters (endorphins) are amino acid chains. Monoamine neurotransmitters (serotonin, dopamine, epinephrine) have an amino group connected to an aromatic ring and regulate attention, cognition, and emotion.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q40: Where are GABA and glycine primarily found, and what is their function?
**Answer:** GABA is the most common inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain, while glycine is the most common inhibitory neurotransmitter in the spinal cord. Both cause hyperpolarization of the postsynaptic membrane, inhibiting neural transmission.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q41: What are the structural components of catecholamine neurotransmitters like dopamine, epinephrine, and norepinephrine?
**Answer:** Catecholamines contain a benzene ring with two hydroxyl groups attached to it. This catechol structure is the defining feature of dopamine, epinephrine (adrenaline), and norepinephrine (noradrenaline).
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q42: How do ionotropic and metabotropic receptors differ in their mechanism and speed of response?
**Answer:** Ionotropic receptors are ligand-gated ion channels that cause fast, direct graded potentials when neurotransmitters bind. Metabotropic receptors use slower second messenger systems but can produce larger, more sustained responses through signal amplification.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q43: What are the four main mechanisms for removing neurotransmitters from the synaptic cleft?
**Answer:** The four mechanisms are: 1) Diffusion away when concentrations are low, 2) Enzymatic breakdown into component parts, 3) Reuptake pumps that reabsorb neurotransmitter back into the presynaptic terminal, and 4) Uptake by astrocytes.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q44: What is the functional relationship between the hypothalamus and pituitary gland?
**Answer:** The hypothalamus acts as the control system that funnels signals to the pituitary gland, which is the 'master gland.' The hypothalamus communicates with the anterior pituitary through the hypophyseal portal system and directly stores hormones in the posterior pituitary.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q45: What does the mnemonic 'FLAT PEG' represent for anterior pituitary hormones?
**Answer:** FLAT represents tropic hormones that stimulate other endocrine glands: FSH, LH, ACTH, and TSH. PEG represents direct hormones that directly stimulate body parts: Prolactin, Endorphins, and Growth hormone.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q46: How do the adrenal cortex and medulla differ in their hormone production?
**Answer:** The adrenal cortex (outer layer) produces steroid hormones like cortisol and aldosterone. The adrenal medulla (inner layer) produces catecholamines like epinephrine and norepinephrine.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q47: What are the differences between autocrine, paracrine, and endocrine signaling?
**Answer:** Autocrine signaling affects the same cell that produces the signal. Paracrine signaling acts regionally on nearby cells. Endocrine signaling acts at a distance through the bloodstream to reach target organs.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q48: Which hypothalamic releasing hormone stimulates ACTH release, and what is ACTH's target?
**Answer:** Corticotropin releasing hormone (CRH) stimulates the anterior pituitary to release ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone). ACTH then travels to the adrenal glands to stimulate hormone release.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q49: What is the mechanism of action for protein/polypeptide hormones versus steroid hormones?
**Answer:** Protein/polypeptide hormones bind to receptors on the cell surface and use secondary messengers to trigger intracellular responses. Steroid hormones pass through the cell membrane and bind to intracellular receptors, acting as primary messengers that directly affect transcription and translation.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q50: How are hormone concentrations regulated in the body?
**Answer:** Hormone concentrations are controlled through metabolism in the liver, kidneys, blood, and sweat. They are also regulated through negative feedback loops that maintain homeostasis by inhibiting further hormone release when target levels are reached.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q51: What distinguishes endocrine from exocrine glands, and how can an organ be both?
**Answer:** Endocrine glands are ductless and secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream, while exocrine glands secrete products into ducts. The liver is both: it's exocrine when secreting bile into ducts for fat emulsification, and endocrine when secreting hormones like insulin-like growth factor into the bloodstream.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q52: Describe the pressure and volume changes during inspiration and expiration.
**Answer:** Inspiration: muscles contract, diaphragm moves down, volume increases, pressure decreases below atmospheric (negative pressure), air flows in. Expiration: muscles relax, alveoli recoil, volume decreases, pressure increases above atmospheric (positive pressure), air flows out.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q53: What is hematocrit and how is it calculated?
**Answer:** Hematocrit is the proportion of red blood cells in blood, calculated as the volume of RBCs divided by the total volume of blood. It's an important measure of blood composition that can indicate conditions like anemia or polycythemia.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q54: What is the difference between polycythemia and anemia?
**Answer:** Polycythemia is a condition with abnormally high levels of red blood cells, while anemia is characterized by abnormally low levels of red blood cells. Both conditions affect oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q55: Explain the Bohr effect and how it relates to oxygen transport.
**Answer:** The Bohr effect describes how increased CO2 and H+ concentrations decrease hemoglobin's affinity for oxygen, promoting oxygen release in metabolically active tissues. This allows efficient oxygen delivery where it's needed most.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q56: What is the Haldane effect and how does it complement the Bohr effect?
**Answer:** The Haldane effect describes how oxygen binding to hemoglobin decreases its affinity for CO2 and H+. This promotes CO2 release in the lungs when oxygen is picked up, working opposite to the Bohr effect for efficient gas exchange.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q57: Describe the role of fibrin in blood clotting and how it's activated.
**Answer:** Fibrin strengthens platelet plugs by forming reinforcing strands. Inactive fibrinogen circulates in blood and is converted to active fibrin by thrombin, which cleaves the inactive portion. This occurs when tissue factor is exposed at vessel injury sites.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q58: What stimulates erythropoietin (EPO) release and what is its function?
**Answer:** Low oxygen levels stimulate EPO release from the kidneys. EPO is a hormone that promotes red blood cell production (erythropoiesis) in the bone marrow, increasing oxygen-carrying capacity when needed.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q59: What are chemokines and how do they differ from other cytokines?
**Answer:** Chemokines are a specific type of cytokine released by infected cells to initiate immune responses and warn neighboring cells. Unlike general cytokines used for cell signaling, chemokines specifically trigger cell trafficking to move immune cells to infection sites.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q60: What are the main components of the innate immune system's physical barriers?
**Answer:** Physical barriers include skin, GI tract, respiratory tract, nasopharynx, cilia, eyelashes, and body hair. Defense mechanisms include secretions like mucous, bile, gastric acid, saliva, tears, and sweat that provide nonspecific protection against pathogens.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q61: How does the complement system function as part of innate immunity?
**Answer:** The complement system marks pathogens for destruction and creates holes in pathogen cell membranes. It works as a general immune response alongside inflammation to eliminate foreign substances identified as non-self.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q62: What is the average lifespan of RBCs compared to platelets?
**Answer:** Red blood cells last approximately 120 days on average, while platelets have a much shorter lifespan of only a few days. Both are eventually broken down by the spleen and liver for component recycling.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q63: What are macrophages and what unique ability allows them to hunt pathogens outside the circulatory system?
**Answer:** Macrophages are efficient phagocytic cells that can leave the circulatory system by moving across capillary walls. This allows them to hunt pathogens in tissues outside the bloodstream and release cytokines to recruit other immune cells to infected areas.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q64: How do mast cells contribute to the inflammatory response when activated?
**Answer:** When activated, mast cells release cytokines and granules containing chemical molecules like histamine. These mediators cause blood vessels to dilate, increasing blood flow and cell trafficking to the infection site, creating an inflammatory cascade for wound healing and pathogen defense.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q65: Why are neutrophils typically the first immune cells to arrive at infection sites?
**Answer:** Neutrophils are typically first because the bone marrow produces approximately 100 billion new neutrophils per day, creating a large circulating population. They are phagocytic granulocytes with toxic granules that kill bacteria and fungi on contact.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q66: What distinguishes Natural Killer (NK) cells from other innate immune cells in their mechanism of action?
**Answer:** NK cells do not attack pathogens directly. Instead, they destroy infected host cells to stop the spread of infection, preventing pathogens from using the host cell's machinery for replication.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q67: What is opsonization and how does it enhance immune system function?
**Answer:** Opsonization is a process where foreign particles are marked for phagocytosis by immune cells. It tags infected cells and identifies circulating pathogens with the same antigens, making them easier targets for destruction by phagocytes.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q68: How does the complement system use cell lysis to combat pathogens?
**Answer:** The complement system's proteins puncture the membranes of foreign cells, destroying membrane integrity. This weakens the pathogen's ability to proliferate and helps stop infection spread by compromising cellular function.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q69: What is agglutination and how does it facilitate pathogen destruction?
**Answer:** Agglutination uses antibodies to cluster and bind pathogens together, similar to rounding up cattle. Once pathogens are grouped together, they become easier targets for destruction by other immune system components.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q70: How do eosinophils and basophils differ in their targets but share similar mechanisms?
**Answer:** Both are granulocytes that target multicellular parasites. Eosinophils secrete toxic proteins and free radicals that kill bacteria and parasites, while basophils release histamine to attack parasites and contribute to inflammatory responses.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q71: Where do B cells form and mature, and what happens when they encounter their matching antigen?
**Answer:** B cells form and mature in the bone marrow, then circulate through the lymphatic system. When a naïve B cell encounters an antigen matching its membrane-bound antibody, it rapidly divides to become either memory B cells (which retain the same membrane-bound antibody) or plasma cells (which secrete soluble antibodies).
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q72: What is the key difference between memory B cells and plasma B cells in terms of antibody expression?
**Answer:** Memory B cells express the same membrane-bound antibodies as their parent naïve B cell. Plasma cells produce the same antibody specificity but secrete soluble antibodies rather than expressing membrane-bound ones.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q73: Where do T cells mature and what two selection processes must they pass?
**Answer:** T cells mature in the thymus after migrating from bone marrow. They must pass positive selection (ensuring they bind only to self-MHC molecules) and negative selection (testing self-tolerance to ensure they only bind self-MHC presenting foreign antigens, not self-antigens).
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q74: What are the three types of mature T cells and their primary functions?
**Answer:** Helper T cells (CD4+) activate other immune cells and bind MHC II. Cytotoxic T cells (CD8+) eliminate pathogens and infected cells and bind MHC I. T regulatory cells (CD4+ CD25+) distinguish self from non-self and prevent autoimmune diseases.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q75: What happens to T cells that fail positive or negative selection in the thymus?
**Answer:** T cells that fail positive selection (cannot bind self-MHC molecules or bind non-self molecules) or negative selection (bind self-MHC without foreign antigen presentation) are eliminated by apoptosis. This ensures only properly functioning, self-tolerant T cells mature.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q76: How do CD4 and CD8 receptors relate to MHC molecule recognition?
**Answer:** CD4 receptors bind to MHC II molecules (found on antigen-presenting cells like dendritic cells and macrophages), while CD8 receptors bind to MHC I molecules. T cells express either CD4 or CD8, but never both, determining their functional specialization.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q77: What is the key difference between humoral and cell-mediated immunity in terms of their mechanisms?
**Answer:** Humoral immunity involves free-floating antibodies in bodily fluids that bind to antigens, while cell-mediated immunity is carried out by cytotoxic cells like Helper and Cytotoxic T cells. Humoral immunity can be transferred through antibodies from immune individuals, whereas cell-mediated immunity is transferred through T cells.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q78: Where does the cancerous production of innate immune system cells in myelogenous leukemias most likely occur?
**Answer:** The bone marrow is the most likely site for cancerous production of innate immune system cells in myelogenous leukemias. This is because the bone marrow is the primary site of hematopoiesis where immune cells are produced.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q79: What are the main functions of Helper T cells in the immune response?
**Answer:** Helper T cells promote B lymphocyte activation and proliferation, induce B cell differentiation into plasma cells and memory cells, promote cytotoxic T cell activation, enhance macrophage activity, and attract other immune cells to infection sites. They essentially coordinate and amplify the immune response.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q80: What is the primary function of RNA polymerase II in gene expression?
**Answer:** RNA polymerase II uses a DNA strand as a template to synthesize a complementary mRNA transcript. This process, called transcription, creates portable genes that can travel from the nucleus to ribosomes for protein synthesis.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q81: What are the two main components of mRNA protection during processing, and what is their function?
**Answer:** The 5' cap (single G nucleotide attached to 5' end) and poly-A tail (long sequence of A nucleotides at 3' end) protect mRNA from exonucleases in the cytoplasm that target RNA molecules with exposed 5' ends.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q82: What is the role of spliceosomes in mRNA processing?
**Answer:** Spliceosomes locate introns (non-coding sequences), cut them out of the mRNA transcript, and fuse the remaining exons (coding sequences) back together to create mature mRNA.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q83: What are the three stop codons and the start codon in the genetic code?
**Answer:** Stop codons are UAA, UAG, and UGA, which terminate protein synthesis. The start codon is AUG, which codes for methionine and initiates translation.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q84: Why is the genetic code described as degenerate, and what is the wobble position?
**Answer:** The genetic code is degenerate because multiple codons can code for the same amino acid. The wobble position is the third nucleotide in a codon, which shows flexibility in base pairing and contributes to this redundancy.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q85: How does the wobble hypothesis protect against harmful mutations?
**Answer:** The wobble hypothesis explains that while the first two codon positions are critical for amino acid selection, the third position allows non-standard base pairing. Mutations at the wobble position may still code for the correct amino acid, preventing harmful protein changes.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q86: What is the relationship between codons and anticodons in protein synthesis?
**Answer:** Anticodons are complementary triplet sequences on tRNA that pair with mRNA codons during translation. This pairing ensures the correct amino acid is added to the growing polypeptide chain.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q87: What is the first step in translation initiation and what does the small ribosomal subunit scan for?
**Answer:** The small ribosomal subunit binds to the mRNA near the 5' end and scans for the AUG start codon. This scanning process is essential for identifying the correct starting point for protein synthesis.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q88: Which tRNA initiates protein synthesis and what anticodon does it carry?
**Answer:** The initiator tRNA carries methionine and has the anticodon UAC, which base pairs with the AUG start codon. This tRNA positions itself in the P-site when the large ribosomal subunit joins the complex.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q89: What are the two main structural features of tRNA and their functions?
**Answer:** The amino acid attachment site at the 3' end where aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase attaches specific amino acids, and the anticodon loop containing three nucleotides that base pair with mRNA codons during translation.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q90: What are the three active sites of the ribosome and their functions during translation?
**Answer:** The A (aminoacyl) site where tRNA binds to mRNA codons, the P (peptidyl) site where amino acids are transferred to the growing polypeptide chain, and the E (exit) site where empty tRNAs exit the ribosome.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q91: What is the primary function of snRNA and where does it operate?
**Answer:** snRNA plays a central role in RNA splicing by removing introns from pre-mRNA and joining exons together. It operates as a key component of the spliceosome in the nucleus of eukaryotic cells.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q92: Compare the ribosomal subunit composition between prokaryotes and eukaryotes.
**Answer:** Prokaryotic ribosomes consist of 30S small and 50S large subunits, while eukaryotic ribosomes consist of 40S small and 60S large subunits. Both contain rRNA and associated ribosomal proteins.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q93: What is the primary mechanism by which miRNAs regulate gene expression?
**Answer:** miRNAs bind to complementary sequences on mRNA molecules, usually resulting in mRNA degradation or repression of translation. This allows them to control the levels of specific mRNAs and fine-tune gene expression post-transcriptionally.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q94: How do siRNAs differ from miRNAs in their mechanism of gene silencing?
**Answer:** siRNAs are usually perfectly complementary to their target mRNA, leading directly to mRNA degradation through RNA interference (RNAi). In contrast, miRNAs may have imperfect complementarity and can cause either degradation or translational repression.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q95: What are the main functional roles of long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs)?
**Answer:** lncRNAs regulate gene expression at epigenetic, transcriptional, and post-transcriptional levels. They act as scaffolds, guides, or decoys to modulate protein activity or chromatin structures, and are involved in chromatin remodeling, transcriptional regulation, and splicing.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q96: What is the primary function of small nucleolar RNAs (snoRNAs)?
**Answer:** snoRNAs guide chemical modifications of ribosomal RNAs, including 2'-O-methylation and pseudouridylation. These modifications are critical for proper ribosome function and occur during rRNA maturation and processing in the nucleolus.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q97: What causes a frameshift mutation and what is its consequence?
**Answer:** Frameshift mutations result from insertion or deletion of nucleotides not divisible by 3. This changes the reading frame of the mRNA transcript, typically leading to completely altered amino acid sequences downstream of the mutation.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q98: How do nonsense mutations differ from missense mutations in their effects?
**Answer:** Nonsense mutations create premature stop codons, leading to truncated proteins due to early translation termination. Missense mutations result in single amino acid substitutions, producing full-length proteins with altered sequences.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q99: What is the structure and function of desmosomes in animal tissues?
**Answer:** Desmosomes are protein complexes that provide tensile strength by serving as intercellular junctions between animal cells. They anchor intermediate filaments to cadherins at the plasma membrane, and cadherins from neighboring cells bind to each other extracellularly, creating strong cell-to-cell adhesion.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q100: What is the endothelium and what are its main functions?
**Answer:** The endothelium is a one-cell-thick layer of specialized epithelial cells lining the cardiovascular system interior. It provides a smooth, friction-reducing surface for blood flow and acts as a barrier regulating substance exchange between blood and tissues, including facilitating WBC movement during inflammation.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q101: Describe the three layers of blood vessel walls from innermost to outermost.
**Answer:** Tunica intima (innermost): endothelial cells with basement membrane. Tunica media (middle): smooth muscle layer, larger in arteries with elastin for high pressure. Tunica externa (outermost): proteins and collagen, may contain vasa vasorum (vessels supplying vessel walls) and nerve endings.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q102: What structural difference between arteries and arterioles allows arterioles to create more resistance in blood flow?
**Answer:** Arterioles have lots of smooth muscle and no elastin in their tunica media, unlike arteries which contain elastin. This allows arterioles to contract more effectively and create greater resistance to blood flow.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q103: What is the basic structure of capillaries and how does it differ from arteries and arterioles?
**Answer:** Capillaries consist of a lumen surrounded by a single cell that forms the entire tube wall. Unlike arteries and arterioles, they lack the complex three-layer structure (tunica intima, media, and externa) and are designed for efficient exchange rather than resistance or pressure regulation.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q104: If mRNA levels are high but corresponding protein levels are undetectable, what is the most likely mechanism?
**Answer:** The most likely mechanism is translational repression by miRNA binding to the 3' untranslated region of the mRNA. This prevents translation without destroying the mRNA, explaining why mRNA levels remain high while protein production is blocked.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q105: What makes prions unique as infectious agents, and how do they cause disease?
**Answer:** Prions are misfolded proteins that lack genetic material but act as infectious agents by inducing secondary structure changes in normal proteins. The resulting misfolded proteins have reduced solubility, leading to protein aggregation and disease pathology.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q106: How do enhancers regulate gene expression in eukaryotes?
**Answer:** Enhancers are DNA regulatory elements that bind transcription factors and form loops to contact gene promoters. When regulatory proteins assemble at enhancers, they activate transcription by engaging RNA polymerase, often from great distances away from the target gene.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q107: What is the relationship between gene expression, transcription, and protein production?
**Answer:** Gene expression requires transcription as the first step. When activity or protein production is observed, it implies the gene was expressed through transcription. Reporter genes are commonly used experimental tools to measure gene expression levels.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q108: What is the normal blood pH range and what happens to blood pH when CO2 levels increase?
**Answer:** Normal blood pH is 7.38-7.42. Higher CO2 concentrations increase blood acidity (decrease pH) because CO2 combines with water to form carbonic acid, which dissociates into HCO3- and H+, producing more hydrogen ions.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q109: Which parts of the nephron are responsible for bicarbonate reabsorption and hydrogen ion secretion?
**Answer:** The proximal tubule, distal tubule, and collecting duct all reabsorb bicarbonate and secrete hydrogen ions. The proximal tubule handles initial HCO3- reabsorption and H+ secretion, while the distal tubule and collecting duct provide fine-tuned regulation of acid-base balance.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q110: What are nodes of Ranvier and where are they found?
**Answer:** Nodes of Ranvier are small, regularly spaced sites where the myelin insulation around myelinated neuron axons is interrupted. The myelin layer is nearly continuous along the axon length except at these nodes.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q111: How do nodes of Ranvier increase the speed of neural impulse transmission in myelinated axons?
**Answer:** Nodes of Ranvier increase transmission speed by reducing the number of action potentials needed along the axon length. Voltage-gated Na+ channels are only present at these nodes, allowing the signal to 'jump' from node to node (saltatory conduction) rather than propagating continuously. This reduces the total number of membrane channel openings required.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q112: What pattern does Na+ ion transport follow in myelinated nerve fibers, and why?
**Answer:** Na+ ion transport follows a 'stair-step' pattern with increments of approximately equal size at each node of Ranvier. This occurs because voltage-gated Na+ channels are only located at the nodes, so Na+ influx only happens at these specific points along the axon rather than continuously along the entire fiber length.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q113: A patient has reduced pulmonary blood flow and fluid accumulation in abdomen/legs. What cardiac valve problem would cause these symptoms?
**Answer:** These symptoms indicate reduced blood flow through the tricuspid valve (between right atrium and right ventricle). This decreases right ventricular filling, reducing pulmonary blood flow. Blood backs up in the right atrium and systemic veins, increasing hydrostatic pressure and causing fluid leakage into tissues (edema).
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q114: Describe the key steps of neurulation and the structures formed.
**Answer:** Neurulation begins when the notochord (mesodermal structure) signals the overlying ectoderm to thicken into the neural plate. The neural plate folds inward forming the neural groove, then the neural folds converge to create the neural tube (CNS precursor). The remaining neural fold sections become the neural crest, which migrates to form most of the PNS.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q115: What is the developmental progression from fertilization through gastrulation?
**Answer:** After fertilization, the zygote undergoes cleavage to form the blastula (hollow ball), which becomes the blastocyst with trophoblast and inner cell mass. Gastrulation then transforms this two-layered structure into a three-layered gastrula, beginning with primitive streak formation on the bilaminar disk to facilitate cell migration.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q116: What are the three primary germ layers formed during gastrulation, and what major structures does each give rise to?
**Answer:** Endoderm (innermost) forms digestive/respiratory tract linings and accessory organs like liver and pancreas. Mesoderm (middle) forms circulatory system, muscles, bones, and parts of urinary/reproductive systems. Ectoderm (outermost) forms nervous system and integumentary system including skin, hair, and nails.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q117: How do totipotent, pluripotent, and multipotent stem cells differ in their differentiation potential?
**Answer:** Totipotent cells (zygote to 8-cell stage) can form entire organisms including extraembryonic tissues. Pluripotent cells (inner cell mass) can form any of the three germ layers but not placental structures. Multipotent cells are committed to specific lineages with limited differentiation options.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q118: What is a morphogen and how does it control cell differentiation during embryogenesis?
**Answer:** A morphogen is a signaling molecule that alters cell differentiation in a concentration-dependent manner. It forms gradients through diffusion, degradation, and uptake. Cells closer to the source experience higher concentrations and differentiate differently than cells farther away, controlling organ development and body axis formation.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q119: What structures form from the trophoblast and inner cell mass during blastocyst development?
**Answer:** The trophoblast (outer cell layer) forms the placenta and other extraembryonic structures. The inner cell mass forms the actual embryo and contains pluripotent cells that can differentiate into any of the three primary germ layers.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q120: How does inductive signaling work in embryonic development?
**Answer:** Inductive signaling involves an inducer cell releasing chemical signals that act on competent neighboring cells. These signals regulate gene expression required for cell determination. Competent cells must express appropriate receptors to respond to specific morphogens or signaling molecules.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q121: What are the four main roles of apoptosis in post-implantation embryonic development?
**Answer:** Apoptosis controls cell numbers, eradicates unnecessary structures, sculpts essential structures, and eliminates abnormal, nonfunctional, or misplaced cells. Abnormal activation of apoptosis (such as from oxidative stress) can cause embryonic abnormalities.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q122: What distinguishes slow-twitch (type 1) muscle fibers from fast-twitch fibers in terms of contraction speed and ATP production?
**Answer:** Type 1 fibers contract slowly due to slower ATP hydrolysis by myosin heads and produce ATP via aerobic respiration with many mitochondria. Fast-twitch fibers achieve rapid contraction through faster ATP hydrolysis and use different metabolic pathways for ATP production.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q123: Compare the metabolic characteristics and fatigue resistance of fast glycolytic (type 2X) versus fast oxidative-glycolytic (type 2A) muscle fibers.
**Answer:** Type 2X fibers rely on anaerobic glycolysis, fatigue easily due to H+ accumulation, and have few mitochondria with low myoglobin. Type 2A fibers use both oxidative and glycolytic processes, are moderately fatigue-resistant, and have abundant myoglobin and capillary networks like type 1 fibers.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q124: What are the key components and characteristics of the innate immune system?
**Answer:** The innate immune system includes physical/chemical barriers (skin, mucous membranes, stomach acid) and immune cells (neutrophils, macrophages, NK cells). It is nonspecific, responds immediately to pathogens, and regulates other immune responses through signaling molecules.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q125: What are the two main types of adaptive immune cells, and what specialized cells do they form upon initial pathogen exposure?
**Answer:** The two main types are B cells and T cells. Upon initial exposure to a specific pathogen, some B and T cells divide and differentiate into memory cells, which are stored in lymphoid tissues and can rapidly recognize and respond to future infections by the same pathogen.

### Q197: During an action potential, why does the neuron become positively charged despite being called 'depolarization'? What common MCAT misconception does this address?
**Answer:** Depolarization refers to making the membrane potential LESS negative (closer to 0), not necessarily positive. ## Biochemistry (140 cards)

### Q1: What are the four key starting materials used in gluconeogenesis?

### Q2: How do Gs and Gq proteins differ in their signaling mechanisms?
**Answer:** Gs stimulates adenylate cyclase, increasing cAMP levels in the cell. Gq activates phospholipase C, which causes IP3 to open calcium channels in the ER, increasing intracellular calcium levels.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q3: What is the difference between kinases and phosphorylases in terms of phosphate sources?
**Answer:** Kinases transfer phosphate groups from ATP to molecules, while phosphorylases add phosphate groups using inorganic phosphate as the source. Phosphatases remove phosphate groups from molecules.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q4: What are the structural characteristics and examples of steroid hormones?
**Answer:** Steroid hormones are derived from cholesterol and have a backbone of 3 cyclohexane rings and 1 cyclopentane ring. They can easily cross cell membranes and include cortisol, testosterone, estrogen, and progesterone. Note: ADH is incorrectly listed as a steroid in the text but is actually a peptide hormone.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q5: How do tyrosine derivatives differ in their mechanisms of action?
**Answer:** Tyrosine derivatives have two distinct mechanisms: T3 and T4 (thyroid hormones) act similar to steroids by crossing cell membranes and affecting transcription, while catecholamines act similar to peptides by binding to cell surface receptors and using secondary messengers.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q6: What is the relationship between terpenes and steroid biosynthesis?
**Answer:** Terpenes are lipid molecules made of repeating 5-carbon isoprene units. Squalene, a tetraterpene (8 isoprene units), forms the basis of all steroid molecules in the body and is converted to cholesterol in the liver, which then serves as the precursor for all steroid hormones.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q7: In size exclusion chromatography, what is the relationship between protein size and elution time?
**Answer:** The retention time is inversely proportional to protein size - larger proteins elute first (earlier) while smaller proteins elute later. This occurs because larger molecules are excluded from the porous beads and travel through the column faster, while smaller molecules enter the pores and take longer to exit.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q8: How does ion exchange chromatography separate proteins, and what are the two types of resin beads used?
**Answer:** Ion exchange chromatography separates proteins based on their net charge using charged resin beads. Anion exchange uses positively charged beads to bind negatively charged proteins, while cation exchange uses negatively charged beads to bind positively charged proteins. Bound proteins are eluted by increasing salt concentration or changing pH.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q9: What principle does affinity chromatography use to separate proteins, and how does it achieve high specificity?
**Answer:** Affinity chromatography separates proteins based on their specific interactions with a ligand attached to the chromatography matrix. The ligand (antibody, enzyme substrate, inhibitor, or metal ion) specifically binds to the target protein while non-binding proteins are washed away. Target proteins are then eluted by disrupting the protein-ligand interaction with high salt, pH changes, or competing ligands.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q10: How does protein size affect sedimentation during centrifugation?
**Answer:** Larger proteins have more mass and experience greater centrifugal force, causing them to sediment faster to the bottom of the centrifuge tube. This allows proteins to be separated by size through fractionation into layers or pellets based on their sedimentation rates.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q11: What are Svedberg units (S) and what do they measure?
**Answer:** Svedberg units (S) measure the sedimentation coefficient, which indicates how fast a particle sediments under centrifugal force. Larger proteins generally have higher sedimentation coefficients (higher S values), meaning they sediment faster than smaller proteins.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q12: How does mass spectrometry identify and quantify proteins?
**Answer:** Mass spectrometry ionizes proteins and fragments them into smaller peptides. These peptides are then separated based on their mass-to-charge (m/z) ratio using electric or magnetic fields, allowing for protein identification and quantification.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q13: What advantage does Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) offer for studying protein interactions?
**Answer:** SPR measures molecular interactions (protein-protein, protein-ligand) in real time without requiring labeling of the molecules. This provides a non-invasive method to study binding kinetics and affinities.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q14: How does two-dimensional gel electrophoresis achieve high-resolution protein separation?
**Answer:** Two-dimensional gel electrophoresis combines isoelectric focusing (separates by charge/pH) with SDS-PAGE (separates by size). This dual separation method provides much higher resolution than either technique alone.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q15: What is the catalytic function of rRNA and what is this activity called?
**Answer:** rRNA catalyzes the formation of peptide bonds between amino acids during translation through its peptidyl transferase activity. This makes rRNA a ribozyme - an RNA molecule with catalytic activity.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q16: Why does DNA remain in aqueous solution after membrane lipid extraction?
**Answer:** DNA's negatively charged phosphate groups on the sugar-phosphate backbone interact with the partially positive hydrogen atoms in water through ion-dipole interactions. This creates a hydration shell that keeps DNA soluble in aqueous solutions.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q17: Why must DNA's charge be neutralized during ethanol precipitation, and how do sodium cations accomplish this?
**Answer:** DNA has a negatively charged phosphate backbone that creates a hydration shell in aqueous solution. Ethanol disrupts this hydration shell, allowing sodium cations to interact with and neutralize the negative charges through ionic bonding with phosphate groups. This neutralization makes DNA less hydrophilic and allows it to precipitate from solution.

### Q18: What is Western blotting and what are its key steps?
**Answer:** Western blotting combines protein electrophoresis with antibody binding to identify target proteins with high specificity. ### Q19: What is the purpose of using blocking buffer (BSA or non-fat dry milk) in Western blotting?

### Q20: Describe the antibody detection system used in Western blotting and how it produces a signal.
**Answer:** Western blotting uses a two-antibody system: a primary antibody specific to the target protein, followed by an enzyme-conjugated secondary antibody that binds to the primary antibody. ### Q30: What is the relationship between Vmax, kcat, enzyme concentration, and reaction rate when [S] = Km?

### Q33: What is saponification and what products are formed during basic hydrolysis of triacylglycerols?
**Answer:** Saponification is base-mediated ester hydrolysis of triacylglycerols. It produces glycerol and fatty acid salts (soap). The reaction begins with hydroxide addition to the carbonyl, forming a tetrahedral intermediate, then the fatty acid salt is released when the carbonyl reforms.

**Answer:** Terpenes are steroid precursors made of 5-carbon isoprene units. Monoterpenes contain 2 isoprenes, diterpenes contain 4 isoprenes (two monoterpenes), and triterpenes contain 6 isoprenes (three monoterpenes). Steroids are derived from triterpenes.

### Q37: Why are steroids classified as nonhydrolyzable lipids?
**Answer:** Steroids are nonhydrolyzable lipids because they lack ester or amide linkages that can be broken by hydrolysis. Unlike hydrolyzable lipids (fatty acids, phospholipids) that contain these linkages, steroids consist of fused hydrocarbon rings without hydrolyzable bonds.

### Q38: What is the structural composition of cholesterol and how is it formed?
**Answer:** Cholesterol is formed by cyclization of the triterpene squalene and contains four fused hydrocarbon rings (three 6-membered rings and one 5-membered ring). It serves as the basic steroid structure and is synthesized from six isoprene units.

### Q39: What structural feature makes some lipids non-hydrolyzable?
**Answer:** Non-hydrolyzable lipids lack ester or amide linkages. Without these functional groups, they cannot undergo hydrolysis reactions that would break them down into smaller components.

### Q40: How are reactive oxygen species (ROS) naturally generated in cells, and how are they neutralized?

### Q43: What is the mechanism by which epinephrine stimulates glycogenolysis in muscle cells?

### Q44: How does glycogen phosphorylase break down glycogen, and what product is formed?

### Q45: How does malonyl-CoA regulate fatty acid metabolism and beta-oxidation?
**Answer:** Malonyl-CoA inhibits long-chain fatty acid transport into mitochondria, thereby decreasing beta-oxidation. When malonyl-CoA levels decrease, more fatty acids can enter mitochondria and beta-oxidation increases.

### Q46: What is the relationship between R/S configuration and D/L sugar designation?

### Q47: What structural feature allows a sugar to be classified as a reducing sugar?

### Q48: How do ketoses undergo oxidation in the Tollens test?

### Q49: Which carbohydrates are always considered reducing sugars?
**Answer:** All monosaccharides, both aldoses and ketoses, are reducing sugars. Disaccharides and oligosaccharides are reducing only if one of their anomeric carbons is free (not involved in a glycosidic bond).

### Q50: What is the difference between allosteric effectors and cofactors in terms of initial enzymatic activity?
**Answer:** Allosteric effectors regulate enzymes that can have zero or nonzero initial activity by binding at sites other than the active site. Cofactors (metal ions and coenzymes) are required for enzymatic activity, meaning the initial activity must be zero without them.

### Q51: How do glycogen phosphorylase and debranching enzyme work together in glycogen breakdown?
**Answer:** Phosphorylase releases glucose residues from the nonreducing ends of linear glycogen regions. Debranching enzyme linearizes branched regions by repositioning them onto short linear segments and releases one glucose residue from each branch.

### Q52: What type of reactions do ligase enzymes catalyze and what energy source do they use?
**Answer:** Ligase enzymes catalyze addition or synthesis reactions between two molecules by coupling them with the hydrolysis of energy-rich bonds, such as those between phosphate groups of nucleotides like ATP.

### Q53: How many acetyl-CoA units are produced from an even-chain fatty acid during β-oxidation?
**Answer:** Even-chain fatty acids yield half as many acetyl-CoA units as the total number of carbons in the original molecule. For example, an 18-carbon fatty acid produces 9 acetyl-CoA units.

### Q55: What structural difference requires unsaturated fatty acids to undergo isomerization during β-oxidation?
**Answer:** Unsaturated fatty acids have C=C double bonds in cis-conformation, but β-oxidation requires trans-bonds. Therefore, cis-bonds must be isomerized to trans-bonds as part of the oxidation mechanism.

### Q56: What two key conditions must be met when determining Vmax and Km using Michaelis-Menten kinetics?
**Answer:** Scientists must measure only the initial reaction rate for each substrate concentration and ensure that each initial substrate concentration tested is much greater than the enzyme concentration.

### Q57: What three key assumptions must hold true for Michaelis-Menten kinetics to be valid, and when do they apply?
**Answer:** The three assumptions are: 1) Constant substrate concentration [S] during reaction, 2) Steady state assumption where [ES] remains constant, and 3) Irreversibility assumption where product doesn't convert back to substrate. All three assumptions only hold true during the initial phase of the reaction, before substrate depletion or product accumulation occurs.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q58: Why must enzyme concentration be much lower than substrate concentration in kinetic studies?
**Answer:** If enzyme concentration is too high relative to substrate, the formation of enzyme-substrate complexes (ES) will significantly deplete the free substrate concentration [S]. This violates the assumption that [S] remains constant during the reaction, which is essential for accurate Michaelis-Menten kinetics measurements.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q59: What types of bonds stabilize protein secondary structure, and what structures do they form?
**Answer:** Hydrogen bonds between backbone carbonyl oxygen and backbone amide hydrogen atoms stabilize secondary structure. These hydrogen bonds bend the polypeptide chain into α-helices and β-pleated sheets, which are the main components of secondary structure.

### Q60: What forces and bonds stabilize protein tertiary structure?
**Answer:** Tertiary structure is stabilized by van der Waals forces, hydrogen bonding between side chains, ionic bonds (salt bridges) between acidic and basic side chains, and disulfide bonds between cysteine residues. The folding is initially driven by hydrophilic and hydrophobic interactions.

### Q61: What is the difference between tertiary and quaternary protein structure in terms of bonds involved?
**Answer:** Tertiary structure involves the 3D folding of a single polypeptide chain, while quaternary structure involves the assembly of two or more polypeptide subunits. ### Q62: What level of protein structure is maintained by peptide bonds, and what happens during complete denaturation?
**Answer:** Primary protein structure is maintained by peptide bonds (covalent bonds between amino acids). During complete denaturation, all levels of protein structure except primary structure are disrupted, meaning secondary, tertiary, and quaternary structures are lost while the amino acid sequence remains intact.

### Q63: How does Le Chatelier's principle explain why the thermodynamically unfavorable cleavage of F-1,6-BP proceeds forward in glycolysis?

### Q66: What are the two main mechanisms by which enzymes catalyze two-substrate reactions?

### Q68: Why are transition state analogs highly potent competitive inhibitors?

### Q71: What is the relationship between D-sugars and L-sugars?

### Q78: What determines whether a bond is thermodynamically stable versus kinetically stable?

**Answer:** Peptide bonds are thermodynamically unstable because their formation requires energy input (GTP hydrolysis), meaning they can break spontaneously. ### Q80: What is the difference between thermodynamic stability and kinetic stability of peptide bonds?

**Answer:** Larger proteins are excluded from entering the porous beads due to size restrictions, so they travel around the beads through the void volume and elute first. This is why fructose (a ketose) is still a reducing sugar - the ketone at C2 can tautomerize through an enediol intermediate to form an aldehyde that reduces Tollens reagent.

### Q107: If glycogen phosphorylase and debranching enzyme are both active, what determines which glucose residues are released as glucose-1-phosphate versus free glucose?
**Answer:** Glycogen phosphorylase releases glucose as G1P from α(1→4) linkages at nonreducing ends. Debranching enzyme releases glucose as FREE glucose only when it cleaves α(1→6) branch points AND repositions the branch. The key difference: phosphorylase acts on linear regions (producing G1P), while debranching enzyme produces free glucose only from the single glucose residue at each α(1→6) branch point during the transfer/hydrolysis process.

### Q108: Compare the reducing sugar properties of maltose versus sucrose. What structural difference accounts for their different behaviors in the Tollens test?
**Answer:** Maltose is reducing (positive Tollens test) because it has one free anomeric carbon that can open and close, allowing oxidation. Sucrose is non-reducing (negative Tollens test) because BOTH anomeric carbons are involved in the glycosidic bond (α1→β2 linkage between glucose C1 and fructose C2). With no free anomeric carbon, sucrose cannot linearize and therefore cannot be oxidized by Tollens reagent.

### Q109: What is the DIFFERENCE between a cofactor and a coenzyme in terms of their chemical nature and requirement for enzymatic activity?
**Answer:** Both cofactors and coenzymes are nonprotein compounds REQUIRED for enzymatic activity (initial activity must be zero without them). The key similarity is that both are essential - the enzyme has NO activity without them, not just reduced activity.

### Q110: A researcher measures enzyme kinetics but observes that Km appears to increase over time during each individual reaction. What is the most likely violation of Michaelis-Menten assumptions?

### Q111: What is the DIFFERENCE between how even-chain and odd-chain fatty acids are processed in β-oxidation?

### Q113: Which enzyme class would be MOST likely to require ATP hydrolysis: ligases, lyases, or transferases?
**Answer:** Ligases are most likely to require ATP hydrolysis. They catalyze synthesis reactions between two molecules by coupling bond formation with the hydrolysis of energy-rich bonds (like ATP). **Answer:** The fatty acid had 18 carbons (including the carboxylic acid carbon) and was even-chained. For even-chain fatty acids: acetyl-CoA units = total carbons/2, so 9 × 2 = 18 carbons. No propionyl-CoA confirms it was even-chained (odd-chain would produce propionyl-CoA).

### Q115: What is the DIFFERENCE between how antibodies recognize epitopes versus how enzymes recognize substrates in terms of binding interactions?
**Answer:** Both use noncovalent interactions (hydrogen bonds, electrostatic forces, van der Waals), but antibodies bind for recognition/marking purposes while enzymes bind to catalyze chemical changes. ### Q116: In Michaelis-Menten kinetics, why must [E]total << [S] for ALL substrate concentrations tested, not just high concentrations?
**Answer:** This ensures the free ligand approximation holds: [S] remains essentially constant even when enzyme-substrate complexes form. If [E] is comparable to [S], significant substrate gets 'tied up' in ES complexes, making the actual free [S] much lower than the added [S], invalidating kinetic measurements.

### Q117: What is the DIFFERENCE between the forces stabilizing secondary structure versus tertiary structure in proteins?
**Answer:** Secondary structure is stabilized primarily by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms (carbonyl O and amide H). **Answer:** In ternary complex mechanisms, both substrates bind simultaneously to form a three-molecule complex before catalysis occurs. ### Q1: Using the thin lens equation, what is the image distance when an object is at infinity?
**Answer:** When an object is at infinity (1/o = 0), the thin lens equation 1/f = 1/o + 1/i becomes 1/f = 1/i. Therefore, the image distance equals the focal length (i = f).
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q2: How do paramagnetic and diamagnetic materials respond to magnetic fields?
**Answer:** Paramagnetic materials have unpaired electrons that align with parallel spins in magnetic fields, making them weakly attracted. Diamagnetic materials have no unpaired electrons and are weakly repelled by magnetic fields.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q3: What is the formula for magnetic field magnitude around a current-carrying wire, and how do MRI coils generate uniform fields?
**Answer:** The magnetic field magnitude is B = μI / 2πr, where μ is permeability, I is current, and r is distance. MRI coils use sequential conductive coils with current flowing in the same direction (clockwise or counterclockwise) to generate uniform magnetic field lines within the coil interior.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q4: How do you calculate the strength of a lens in diopters, and what happens when you combine two lenses?
**Answer:** Lens strength S = 1/f, where f is the focal length in meters, giving strength in diopters (D). When combining two lenses with strengths S1 and S2, the total strength equals S1 + S2.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q5: Why does a glass bulb float in a Galileo thermometer when the temperature decreases?
**Answer:** As temperature decreases, the liquid in the cylinder contracts and its density increases. The glass bulb, which has a fixed density, becomes relatively less dense than the surrounding liquid and therefore floats due to increased buoyant force.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q6: How does myopia affect light focusing, and what type of lens corrects this condition?
**Answer:** Myopia (nearsightedness) occurs when light from distant objects focuses in front of the retina instead of directly on it. This condition is corrected using a diverging lens, which creates virtual, upright images and helps focus light properly on the retina.
**Difficulty:** easy

### Q7: What is hyperopia and how is it corrected?
**Answer:** Hyperopia is farsightedness where light rays from nearby objects focus beyond the retina. It is corrected using converging lenses that bend light rays inward before they reach the eye, creating a real and inverted image.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q8: What is dispersion and why does it occur?
**Answer:** Dispersion is the phenomenon of light separating into its colors when passing through a medium. It occurs because different wavelengths of light have slightly different refractive indices in the same medium, causing different angles of refraction for different colors.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q9: What is chromatic aberration and why does violet light blur more than red light?
**Answer:** Chromatic aberration is the formation of blurry images due to dispersion through a lens. Violet light has a shorter wavelength and higher frequency than red light, causing it to refract more and converge before the screen, resulting in a blurry image.
**Difficulty:** hard

### Q10: What is refraction and how does the refractive index affect the direction of bending?
**Answer:** Refraction is the bending of light rays when crossing between media with different refractive indices. If the second medium has a higher refractive index, light bends toward the normal; if lower, it bends away from the normal.
**Difficulty:** medium

### Q11: How do focal length signs and magnification signs relate to lens and image properties?

### Q13: What is the difference between convex and concave lenses in terms of light behavior?

### Q104: If you have a solution containing both AgCl and NaCl, what happens to AgCl solubility due to the common ion effect, and how would this change if temperature increased?
**Answer:** The Cl⁻ from NaCl shifts the AgCl equilibrium left, decreasing AgCl solubility (common ion effect). ### Q105: What is the fundamental difference between how reducing agents and oxidizing agents behave in redox reactions, and why is this counterintuitive?
**Answer:** Reducing agents cause reduction in other species but get oxidized themselves; oxidizing agents cause oxidation in others but get reduced themselves. The agent 'sacrifices' itself (undergoes the opposite process) to cause the named process in another species.

### Q106: In Fischer esterification, which reactant provides the alkyl oxygen that becomes bonded to the carbonyl carbon in the product ester, and why is this mechanistically significant?

### Q110: Compare SN1 vs SN2 reaction mechanisms: If you have a tertiary alkyl halide with a strong nucleophile, which mechanism predominates and why?

### Q111: Rank the following in order of INCREASING nucleophilicity in a protic solvent: F⁻, Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻. What is the underlying principle?
**Answer:** F⁻ < Cl⁻ < Br⁻ < I⁻ (increasing nucleophilicity). ### Q112: A carbocation intermediate has an electron-withdrawing NO2 group attached to the adjacent carbon. How does this affect carbocation stability compared to an alkyl-substituted carbocation?
**Answer:** The NO2 group DESTABILIZES the carbocation through the inductive effect. The highly electronegative nitrogen and oxygens pull electron density away from the positively charged carbon, creating adjacent partial positive charges. This is unfavorable compared to alkyl groups, which donate electron density through hyperconjugation and inductive effects, stabilizing the positive charge.

### Q113: What is the mechanistic difference between how electron-donating groups affect carbocation stability versus how they affect nucleophilicity?
**Answer:** For carbocation stability: EDGs donate electrons through inductive effects and hyperconjugation, directly stabilizing the positive charge. Both involve electron donation but to different species - the carbocation versus the attacking nucleophile.

### Q114: What is the DIFFERENCE between how temperature affects solubility of solids versus gases, and what is the molecular explanation?
**Answer:** Solid solutes: solubility INCREASES with temperature (heat provides kinetic energy to overcome intermolecular forces between solute particles). ### Q123: Why would vacuum distillation be preferred over simple distillation for separating heat-sensitive organic compounds, and how does this relate to the relationship between pressure and boiling point?

## General (2 cards)

### Q1: Which of the following scenarios would VIOLATE Weber's Law, and why?
**Answer:** A scenario where the difference threshold remains constant regardless of background intensity would violate Weber's Law. Weber's Law states that ΔI/I = k (constant ratio), meaning larger background intensities require proportionally larger difference thresholds. If ΔI stayed the same while I increased, the ratio would decrease, violating the law. This commonly occurs at very high or very low stimulus intensities where Weber's Law breaks down.
**Difficulty:** Hard

### Q2: If you need to distill a thermally sensitive compound with bp 180°C, why would vacuum distillation be preferred over atmospheric distillation?
**Answer:** Vacuum distillation lowers the boiling point below 150°C threshold, preventing thermal degradation. Reduced pressure decreases vapor pressure required for boiling, allowing distillation at lower, safer temperatures.
**Difficulty:** Advanced
